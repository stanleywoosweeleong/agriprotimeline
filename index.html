How this app tracks the season

1. The calendar is the real clock

For durian, the method science and growers actually rely on is days after anthesis (days from full bloom), confirmed by the physical signs of ripeness on the tree. That is exactly what this timeline does: you anchor a bloom, bud or harvest date and the rail counts the days for your chosen clone. The day-count is the backbone — everything else only refines it.

2. Where GDD fits — and where it doesn't

Growing Degree Days (GDD) accumulate heat instead of days, so a hot spell nudges the projection earlier and a cool one later:

GDD = ( Max Temp + Min Temp ) / 2 − Base Temp

Be clear about the limits, though. There is no peer-reviewed base temperature established for durian — the figure below is a working assumption. And in stable equatorial lowlands daily heat barely varies, so GDD tracks the calendar very closely and adds little on flat ground. Its honest job here is a temperature-and-elevation adjustment layered on the day-count — not a validated durian heat model, and never the thing that decides harvest on its own.

3. This app's lowland calibration

This build is tuned for lowland orchards below 900 m. The settings below are model assumptions to refine against your own field records — they are not measured constants.

Base temperature — 10 °C (assumption)

The threshold below which durian development effectively stalls. 10 °C is a working tropical default. Lower it if your trees run "ahead" of the projected dates; raise it if they run behind.

Reference lowland heat — ≈ 17.5 GDD / day

A typical lowland day (≈ 32 °C high, 23 °C low) yields about 17.5 GDD at a 10 °C base. Each clone's GDD target is simply its days-after-anthesis × this figure, which keeps the heat model and the calendar consistent at sea level.

Induction trigger ≠ base temperature

To trigger flowering, agronomists watch for soil temperatures of 25–28 °C alongside daytime air peaks above 32 °C plus drought stress. The 10 °C base is only for accumulating heat units — it is not the flowering trigger.

Field reference for farmers

Watch the shaded soil surface under the canopy. If it reaches 30–32 °C at peak afternoon heat, the soil ~10 cm down has usually hit the 25–28 °C target — a way to gauge induction progress before seeking expert verification.

4. Elevation correction

When you sync a location, the weather service already reports temperatures for that grid cell's elevation. The elevation slider only corrects the residual difference between that grid cell and your actual orchard, using the standard lapse rate of −0.65 °C per 100 m. For most lowland sites this is small or zero — it matters most on hillsides and toward the highland boundary.

5. What actually decides harvest

Durian is harder to call by date than almost any fruit, and the export trade's biggest problem is fruit picked too early. So the timeline tells you when to start checking — the tree makes the final call. The most reliable signs: a corky, swollen stalk and abscission ring; a hollow, loose sound when you tap the fruit; broadened, blunter, wider-spaced thorns; and a deep aroma rising from the base. For an objective lab-grade check, dry-matter content is the gold standard — thresholds are published for Thai Monthong but not most Malaysian clones, so it is worth recording the dry-matter % at which your own trees taste right.

6. The research frontier (not in this app)

Researchers now estimate durian dry matter and maturity non-destructively with near-infrared spectroscopy, density and rind-colour scanning, thermal imaging, and phone-camera AI models. These are promising but need dedicated hardware or a large, locally trained dataset to be accurate. They are deliberately left out here rather than faked — this stays a planning and decision tool, and the harvest call belongs to your eyes, ears and nose at the tree.
